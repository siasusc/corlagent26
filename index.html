Overview

Introduction

Physical AI systems are embodied agents acting in the physical world: robots, vehicles, drones, and other systems that sense, decide, and act under real-world constraints. As these systems scale from isolated robots to deployed populations, interaction becomes a first-order concern rather than a detail of the environment.

Robotaxi fleets, warehouse robots, aerial delivery systems, and collaborative manipulation platforms already reveal population-level phenomena such as congestion, deadlock, oscillation, coordination failure, and social dilemmas. This workshop asks a central question: can the multi-agent problem be deferred until single-agent capability matures, or must it shape physical AI from the beginning?

We bring together researchers and practitioners in robot learning, autonomous driving, warehouse and logistics robotics, aerial systems, collaborative manipulation, foundation models, and multi-agent learning to build a shared map of the challenges and opportunities at this intersection.

We will explore the following focused research questions:

Does single-robot scaling deliver multi-agent competence?

If data and generalist policies produce broad capability, does coordinated population behavior emerge from the same recipe?

Centralized or decentralized at deployment scale?

When is a robot population best treated as one high-dimensional controller, and when is it genuinely a multi-agent system?

Coordination under misaligned objectives

What can incentive design, mechanism design, or light-touch control achieve when agents with different owners share physical space?

Simulation, benchmarks, and sim-to-real for populations

What simulators, metrics, and transfer methods let the community measure population-scale safety, throughput, and emergent behavior?

Invited Speakers and Panelists

Sergey Levine

UC Berkeley / Physical Intelligence

Sergey Levine anchors the single-robot scaling perspective. His work on robot learning, reinforcement learning, and generalist policies helps frame whether scaling data and models can produce multi-agent competence, or whether interaction must be treated as a distinct design axis.

Peter Stone

Professor, UT Austin / Sony AI

Peter Stone brings foundations of multi-agent systems, including teamwork, learning, and coordination among agents without complete shared information. His perspective connects core multi-agent theory with deployment-facing questions in robotics and AI systems.

Dorsa Sadigh

Associate Professor, Stanford University

Dorsa Sadigh works on interaction-aware robotics, human-robot interaction, and coordination with agents whose objectives may differ. Her perspective is central to understanding shared physical spaces, misaligned objectives, and interactive robot behavior.

Ziwei Liu

Assistant Professor, Nanyang Technological University

Ziwei Liu brings expertise in visual foundation models, generative world models, and embodied AI. His work helps connect large-scale perception and simulation to population-scale physical behavior and multi-agent evaluation.

Call for Papers

We invite short papers on new, preliminary, or in-progress work at the intersection of multi-agent systems and physical AI. The workshop is intended as a venue for early ideas, position papers, benchmarks, systems, and deployment lessons that can help the community understand when multi-agent structure matters for embodied AI.

Areas of Interest

Topics of interest include, but are not limited to:

  • Multi-agent and population-scale robot learning
  • Scaling single-agent robot policies to coordinated population behavior
  • Centralized versus decentralized control for deployed robot populations
  • Coordination, incentives, and mechanism design under misaligned objectives
  • Foundation models, world models, and generalist policies for multi-agent physical AI
  • Simulation, benchmarks, sim-to-real transfer, and fleet-level safety or throughput evaluation

Submission Guidelines

  • Submissions should be up to 4 pages, excluding references.
  • Submissions may describe new results, preliminary work, negative results, benchmarks, systems, or position papers.
  • Reviews will be double-blind and handled through OpenReview.
  • Work already accepted to the main CoRL 2026 conference will not be accepted to the workshop.
  • Accepted papers will be presented as short oral talks or posters. Top-rated accepted papers will be invited for contributed oral presentations.
  • A sponsor-backed best paper award will be presented during the closing session.
